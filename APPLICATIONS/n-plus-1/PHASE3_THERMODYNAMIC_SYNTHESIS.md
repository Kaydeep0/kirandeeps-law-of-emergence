## THE BOUNDARY TEST

### Core Framework from Energy Economics

Every profit claim can be tested by expanding the analytical boundary:

**Level 1 - Entity**: Does the actor gain energy/resources?
**Level 2 - Local System**: Does the immediate ecosystem gain usable energy?
**Level 3 - Wider System**: Does the broader economy/society gain?
**Level 4 - Planetary**: Does the biosphere's usable energy increase?

### Three Types of Profit

| Type | Entity Level | System Level | Example |
|------|--------------|--------------|---------|
| **Value Creation** | Positive | Positive | Beaver builds dam → new ecosystem |
| **Zero-Sum Transfer** | Positive | Zero | Seagull steals fish → redistribution |
| **Extraction** | Positive | Negative | Algae bloom → ecosystem collapse |

**The key insight**: Extraction only *appears* profitable when you keep the boundary narrow.

## THE MISSING VARIABLE: DURATION (D)

### Nature's Mandatory Reset

In natural systems, **death redistributes accumulated energy**:

- A squirrel's nut cache doesn't transfer to offspring
- A beaver's dam doesn't compound for generations
- A lion's territory reverts to competition upon death
- Accumulated fat returns to the food web

**Energy accumulation is reset by mortality.**

This creates a natural ceiling on concentration. No matter how efficient an organism becomes at energy capture, death redistributes the gains.

### Humans Removed This Limit

| Innovation | Effect on D (Duration) |
|------------|----------------------|
| Inheritance customs | Energy transfers 1 generation |
| Written property rights | Energy transfers multiple generations |
| Corporate charters | Energy accumulates in immortal entities |
| Trusts/Foundations | Energy compounds indefinitely |
| Compound interest | Financial claims grow exponentially |

**The death reset has been eliminated.**

### Proposed Addition to Equation

```
Concentration_Rate = (R × I × S × D) / (V × C × F)
```

Where **D = Duration multiplier** (how long accumulated advantage persists without reset)

| Era | D Value | Mechanism |
|-----|---------|-----------|
| Natural | 1 | Death redistributes within 1 generation |
| Medieval | 3-5 | Inheritance, but fragmented by succession |
| Industrial | 10-50 | Trusts, corporate perpetuity |
| Digital | ∞ | Immortal corporations, compound returns, no reset |

**Adding D to the equation explains why concentration accelerates over time even when other variables remain stable.**

## THE JEVONS PARADOX PROBLEM

### The Efficiency Trap

Standard economics assumes: Lower costs → Less resource use → Sustainability

Reality (Jevons Paradox): Lower costs → More consumption → Faster depletion

**Historical examples:**

| Innovation | Expected Effect | Actual Effect |
|------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Efficient steam engines (1800s) | Less coal use | Coal consumption exploded |
| Fuel-efficient cars (1970s-2000s) | Less gas use | Miles driven increased, total consumption up |
| LED lighting (2010s) | Less electricity | Lights everywhere, total consumption up |
| Cloud computing efficiency | Less energy | Data center energy use growing 15%+ annually |

### Implication for Our Equation

When **C (cost of extraction)** decreases:

**First-order effect**: Extraction becomes cheaper → concentration accelerates
**Second-order effect**: More total extraction occurs → resource depletion accelerates

Lower C doesn't save resources. It accelerates their consumption.

**This means efficiency improvements make the problem worse, not better.**
